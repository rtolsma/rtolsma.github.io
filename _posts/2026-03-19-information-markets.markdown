There's a popular line of reasoning in the prediction markets crowd that goes something like: markets are the most efficient information aggregation mechanism ever devised, and therefore maximizing their reach, liquidity, and accessibility is a clear social good. The casino-ification of everything -- sports, elections, geopolitics, weather -- is framed as a democratization project. Information wants to be free, and markets are the vehicle.

I think this gets something important wrong. Not about markets being useful, but about what happens when you push liquidity and information symmetry to their logical extremes. The optimal amount of friction in an information market is not zero, and the reasons are more structural than most proponents of prediction markets seem willing to engage with.

## The Value of Information is its Asymmetry

Start with a basic observation: information is only valuable insofar as it's asymmetric. If everyone knows something simultaneously, nobody can act on it differentially, and the economic value of that knowledge collapses to zero[^1]. This is just the efficient markets hypothesis taken seriously -- in a world of perfect, instantaneous information dissemination, there are no returns to being informed, because the information is already priced in.

This matters because information asymmetry is not randomly distributed. It is, overwhelmingly, socially and positionally tied to existing structures. The CEO knows about the acquisition before you do. The senator's aide has advance notice of the policy shift. The quant fund has the co-located server and the proprietary alternative data feed. Access to information is, and always has been, a function of where you sit in existing hierarchies of power, capital, and social connection.

So when we say the value of information is proportional to its asymmetry, we're also implicitly saying that the returns to information accrue disproportionately to those who already occupy positions of structural advantage. This is not a bug in how information markets work -- it's the core mechanism. And it means that making information _more_ valuable by increasing market completeness and liquidity can, counterintuitively, reinforce existing power structures rather than disrupt them.

## Friction as a Feature

The regulatory apparatus around insider trading, material nonpublic information, and trading halts is usually discussed as a necessary evil -- a cost we pay to maintain some baseline of fairness that prevents total capture of market returns by insiders. But I'd argue the framing should be stronger than that. These frictions aren't just costs we tolerate; they're costs society should actively want to pay, because they directly reduce the outsized returns to existing information asymmetry.

Consider what insider trading regulations actually accomplish: they impose a tax on the speed at which positional information advantages can be monetized. By forcing a delay between acquisition of material information and the ability to trade on it, you compress the window during which asymmetric information generates returns. The information still gets priced in eventually, but the rents extracted by those with first access are reduced. This is, effectively, a transfer from the informationally privileged to the informationally average[^2].

From a pure efficiency standpoint, this is costly -- prices take longer to incorporate new information, capital doesn't flow as quickly to its theoretically optimal allocation. But efficiency in the Pareto sense has never been the only thing we optimize for in market design, and the distributional consequences of who captures the surplus from information incorporation are worth taking seriously.

But here's the thing: even without regulation, information doesn't actually diffuse instantaneously in the real world. It moves through networks of people, institutions, journalists, analysts -- each with their own latencies and filters. The delta between a market with some friction and one with perfect instantaneous liquidity is, in terms of social surplus from information access, just not that large. Markets with a small amount of friction still price information in over hours or days. The social cost of that delay compared to microsecond incorporation is negligible for almost anyone who isn't running a trading strategy. The people for whom the difference matters are, almost by definition, the ones extracting rents from speed advantages in the first place.

## The Zero-Sum Problem

Here's where prediction markets specifically start to fall apart relative to the pitch their operators make. The platforms -- Polymarket, Kalshi, and the rest -- sell themselves as democratizing information. They frame the product as a public good: instead of information being locked up in the heads of insiders and experts, it gets aggregated into a publicly visible price signal that everyone can benefit from. Markets as infrastructure for truth.

But prediction markets, unlike equity markets, are zero-sum games. Every dollar won by one participant is a dollar lost by another. There is no underlying productive enterprise generating surplus. No earnings, no dividends, no growth in intrinsic value over time. The entire game is a redistribution of the pot among participants based on who had better information or better models.

In a zero-sum game, the only source of returns is information asymmetry. If you have no edge, your expected return is negative (after fees and spreads). This means that democratizing _participation_, absent democratizing the actual underlying information, just creates a larger pool of participants for the informationally advantaged to extract from. The recreational bettor on Polymarket is not participating in price discovery in any meaningful sense -- they're providing liquidity for sharper participants to trade against[^3].

This is the tension that the platforms don't address. They'll point to the accuracy of prediction market prices as evidence that the product works, and sure, the prices _are_ informative. But the mechanism by which they become informative is the systematic transfer of wealth from less-informed to more-informed participants. The information is democratized in the sense that anyone can _read_ the price, but the process of _producing_ that price is extractive. The marketplace captures fees on both sides. The sharp money captures edge. The average participant provides the subsidy for both.

## Why Traditional Markets are Different

Traditional equity and debt markets are also riddled with information asymmetry, and insiders absolutely extract rents from their positional advantages. But there's a difference that matters: these markets are not zero-sum. Companies produce goods, generate revenue, grow, and create real economic surplus. When a retail investor buys an index fund, they're participating in the compounding of that surplus over time, regardless of whether they possess any informational edge.

This is why it's still broadly good to democratize participation in equity markets, even knowing that sophisticated participants will capture disproportionate short-term returns from information advantages. The long-run return to being invested in the productive capacity of the economy is positive, and broadening access to that return is equalizing. The informational extraction is a drag, but it's a drag on a positive-sum game, and the net outcome for participants is still typically positive.

The mistake is in generalizing this to all market structures. "Markets are good, therefore more markets are better" only holds when the underlying game is positive-sum. Extending it to zero-sum prediction markets and sports betting is a category error that conflates the informational output of markets (prices are accurate!) with the distributional consequences for participants (wealth flows to the informed).

## Information Dissemination Without Markets

There's also a historical point worth making. Society has not, for the vast majority of its existence, relied on markets as its primary mechanism for information dissemination. News, scientific findings, policy developments, cultural shifts -- all of these have been transmitted through journalism, academic publication, government reports, word of mouth, and more recently through social media and digital platforms. Markets reflected this information, but they were downstream of it, not the primary channel.

The idea that markets should _be_ the primary channel for information transmission is quite new. High-frequency trading, the real-time pricing of everything from political outcomes to celebrity gossip, the gamification of news events into tradeable contracts -- this entire ecosystem is really a product of the last fifteen to twenty years, arguably less. And the marginal value of market-mediated information aggregation, above what traditional dissemination channels already provide, is small in most cases. For the kinds of information that matter most to people's daily lives -- policy changes, economic conditions, public health -- traditional news and institutional reporting already do a reasonable job. The incremental improvement from a real-time price signal is modest at best, which connects back to the point about friction: if the social value of information is roughly the same whether it diffuses over hours versus milliseconds, then the case for maximizing market liquidity as a public good collapses. What you're left with is a speed advantage that benefits a narrow set of participants at the expense of everyone else.

## So What

I'm not saying all prediction markets are bad, or that information frictions should be maximized. Some level of market-mediated price discovery is valuable, and there are specific domains where prediction markets are useful for aggregating dispersed private information. But the assumption that more liquidity, less friction, and broader participation are always better deserves more scrutiny than it gets.

The value of information is its asymmetry. Information asymmetry is structurally correlated with existing power. Markets that exist solely to monetize information asymmetry in zero-sum games are, by construction, mechanisms for the informationally privileged to extract from everyone else. And the friction we impose on these dynamics through regulation isn't just a cost -- it's one of the few tools we have to prevent the returns to being informed from compounding indefinitely into the hands of those already advantaged.

Sometimes, the optimal amount of illiquidity is not zero.
